Systematic Process to Analysis Data

  • Data Collection: The relevant data points are gathered at consistent intervals, ensuring accuracy and completeness.
  • Data Processing: Cleaning, organizing, and transforming the data to remove errors, handle missing values, and make it ready for analysis.
  • Data Visualization: Using visualization techniques like line charts, scatter plots, or heatmaps to identify trends, patterns, seasonality, or irregularities.
  • Stationarity/Non-Stationarity: Ensuring that the data is stationary (constant mean and variance over time) for easier modeling. If the data is non-stationary, transformations like differencing or detrending might be required.
  • Time Series Modeling: Applying models like ARIMA (Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average), SARIMA (Seasonal ARIMA), or LSTMs to capture patterns and make predictions.
  • Evaluation and Validation: Assessing model performance using train-test splits or cross-validation to ensure accurate and reliable predictions.

Components of Time Series

In time series analysis, the components are the underlying structures or patterns that describe the overall behavior of the data over time.

Now let’s see the main components of time series analysis:

  • Trend
  • Seasonality
  • Cyclicals
  • Irregular/Random (Noise)

Trend

Trend is like the spine of time series. It indicates the general direction toward which something is heading over a long time. A trend is a common phenomenon in time series data when it tends to increase, decrease, or stay constant over time. It is what the data looks like overall when smaller fluctuations are disregarded.

Types of Trends:
  • Upward Trend: When data is consistently growing over time. Example: The profits of a company were 300 lakh in 1983 and increased to 900 lakh in 2012.
  • Decreasing Trend: When data is going down over time. Example: The decline of old technology like ringtones or pagers over the years.
  • No Trend: When data stays the same or changes randomly.
How Can We Measure Trend Effect?

There are several methods to identify and analyze trends:

  • Freehand Curve Method: A graphical method, where we plot the data and draw a smooth curve that represents the trend.
  • Method of Selected Points: Choosing specific points from the dataset and using them to analyze the trend.
  • Method of Semi-Averages: Dividing the dataset into two equal parts, calculating averages for each, and then determining the trend.
  • Moving Averages Method: Smoothing the data by taking averages over a fixed period of time.
  • Method of Least Squares: A mathematical method where a straight line is fit to best represent the data.

Seasonality

Some seasons are cold, others are warm — we experience different seasonality. So do datasets. Seasonal effects show patterns that repeat over specific time periods like weeks, months, or even years. For example, sales of air coolers rise during the summer and fall during winter. This pattern repeats every year and is a clear example of seasonal effects.

Cyclical Effect

When a business or industry goes through ups and downs, it may follow a cycle. Cyclical patterns have phases like:

  • Prosperity (growth)
  • Recession (slowdown)
  • Depression (bottoming out)
  • Recovery (comeback)

Cyclical effects repeat over time but not as regularly as seasonal effects. They are often linked to economic or business phases. For example, the number of employees in the software industry between 1988 and 2012 may show a cyclical pattern due to economic booms, recessions, and recoveries.

Irregular Effect

Irregular effects are surprises or random events that happen unexpectedly. For example, the pandemic or extreme weather conditions like floods can cause irregular effects. These events are unpredictable but impact the data, causing spikes or dips.

Example: In the air cooler sales scenario, a sudden heatwave could lead to a spike in sales.

Time Series Models

Time series models are analytical tools that predict the future based on past data points ordered chronologically. These models are classified into two types:

  • Classical Models/Traditional Models
  • Advanced Models

Classical Models

  • ARIMA: Ideal for stationary data, using past values (autoregression), differencing to stabilize trends, and moving averages to smooth noise.
  • Exponential Smoothing: Includes Simple (SES), Double (Holt’s), and Triple (Holt-Winters) methods for forecasting with or without trends and seasonality.
  • Vector Autoregression (VAR): Analyzes interdependencies between variables by considering their own lagged values and those of other variables.
  • Seasonal Time Series Decomposition (STL): Breaks data into seasonal, trend, and residual components for deeper insights.

Advanced Models

  • Tree-Based Methods: Random Forests and Gradient Boosting (e.g., XGBoost, LightGBM) handle non-linear relationships and high-dimensional datasets.
  • CatBoost: Optimized for categorical and numerical time series with built-in handling for categorical variables.
  • Neural Networks:
    • Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs): Capture temporal dependencies using feedback connections. Variants like LSTM and GRU address long sequences.
    • Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs): Detect local patterns or anomalies, often combined with RNNs for spatial-temporal features.
    • Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs): Tailored for time series, offering better performance on long sequences.
    • Transformers: Use self-attention mechanisms to identify dependencies across time steps. Examples include Time-Series Transformer and Informer.
  • Support Vector Machines (SVMs): Effective for regression and classification tasks, especially for datasets with clear decision boundaries.

Furthermore, there are instances where it appears that the model predictions do not match the actual results. This can happen because sudden changes are more difficult to predict without more information, and LSTMs forecast the next value based on historical data. Due to the intrinsic unpredictability of some patterns in the data, a lack of training data, or a model with insufficient capacity (such as the number of neurons or layers), the model may not be able to capture these abrupt changes.

We could utilize extra engineering characteristics like rolling averages or volatility measurements(this is more advanced), add more neurons or layers to the LSTM to make it more complicated, or even include external elements (like market movements) to make the model better.
